Abstract

This paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of end-to-end structured extraction for technical datasheets, comparing three distinct approaches: human-assisted annotation, a fully automated pipeline, and a zero-shot method using Large Language Models (LLMs).

The study introduces two novel datasets to benchmark each method's accuracy, efficiency, and robustness, quantifying trade-offs between human effort, automation, and LLM performance in handling industrial documentation complexities.

Technical datasheets in manufacturing are typically distributed as unstructured PDFs with heterogeneous formats, requiring costly manual processing. This research addresses the critical shift from basic extraction to complex semantic structuring needed for industrial applications.

Methodology

Three Extraction Approaches

Human-Assisted Annotation
Combines human expertise with semi-automatic tools for annotation of technical datasheets. The annotator interacts with a dedicated interface that integrates OCR, entity recognition models, and algorithms for converting labeled text into structured formats.
Fully Automated Pipeline
Utilizes OCR, Named Entity Recognition (NER), and text-to-structure conversion without human supervision. The system autonomously performs OCR, identifies and classifies relevant entities, and structures the data into the target format.
Zero-Shot LLM-based Extraction
Leverages large language models with carefully designed prompts for end-to-end extraction. This method requires no prior fine-tuning, training data, or pipeline configuration.

Datasets

The research utilized two dedicated datasets:

  • Single products dataset: 101 pages from 42 technical datasheets (19 manufacturers), containing over 10,000 words
  • Catalogues dataset: 50 pages from 21 documents (13 brands), containing over 15,000 words with multiple products per page

These datasets were designed to reflect the structural and semantic complexity of real-world industrial documentation.

Results

Performance Analysis of Each Approach

Human-Assisted Annotation

Pros: Highest accuracy (99.4%), No hallucinations, Complete control over output quality

Cons: Time-consuming (3 hours per dataset), Labor-intensive, Not scalable for large volumes

Fully Automated Pipeline

Pros: No hallucinations, Consistent performance, No human effort required after setup

Cons: Moderate accuracy (78.27%), Struggles with layout variations, Requires training data

Zero-Shot LLM-based Extraction

Pros: Cost-effective, Highly scalable, No training required, Handles diverse layouts well

Cons: Prone to hallucinations, Truncation issues with large documents, Variable performance across models

Cost Analysis

The study found that LLM-based approaches offer significant cost advantages:

  • Human-assisted annotation: ~$60-90 per dataset (3 hours at $20-30/hour)
  • Gemini 2.5 Pro: $0.45 per dataset
  • Gemini 2.5 Flash: $0.12 per dataset
  • Mistral models: as low as $0.02 per dataset

Conclusion

This study demonstrates that while human-assisted annotation achieves the highest accuracy (99.4%), it is labor-intensive and economically prohibitive at scale. LLM-based extraction reduces human involvement to near zero, delivering substantial savings in both time and cost.

Among LLMs, Gemini 2.5 Pro showed the best performance with the highest overall accuracy (82.57% for single products, 93.07% for catalogues) and relative accuracy (99%, 95.89%). The GPT family exhibited significant limitations, especially with catalogue-style documents where truncation issues led to dramatically lower accuracy.

The findings highlight the current limitations of state-of-the-art models and point toward a future where fully automated structured extraction becomes feasible for industrial applications through improved model robustness and semantically-aware evaluation metrics.
